## TL;DR

I'm joining a company where the systems already exist, the scale is real, and the problems are different. That's the point.

## What I built

At Honeycomb Software I led ~15 engineers – ML, software, and DevOps – building production RAG systems, vectorisation pipelines, and enterprise AI products. Our CV inference pipeline was too slow to run within the SLA. Parts passed uninspected. After targeted optimizations – not a new model, just smarter engineering around the existing one – inference dropped ~40%, and the system caught defects it had been silently missing.

At GR8 Tech, I designed an ML recommendation platform, built MLOps infrastructure from scratch with Kubernetes and Kubeflow, and created a YAML-driven DAG factory that let data scientists ship pipelines without writing code.

At SoftConstruct, I led the team implementing broadcasting for AJNA – a sports AI platform that stitched two 4K camera feeds into a panoramic view. My focus was optimizing the existing football tracking pipeline and delivering real-time HD broadcasts with computer vision.

No existing platform to extend. No established patterns to follow. Just constraints, a team, and a deadline.

I learned where that stops compounding. Every system I've built, team I've led, and constraint I've shipped under becomes the lens for the next decision – and that lens gets sharper faster than any single skill.

## Why the move

Building from scratch teaches you architecture, trade-offs, and how to ship under pressure. But the problems worth solving shift. They stop being "how do I build this?" and become "how do I make this work at scale, inside a living system, with real users and real consequences?"

Monday.com is not a startup looking for someone to build version one. It's a product company with scale, existing infrastructure, and real complexity. The challenge isn't building the system – it's changing it while millions of people are standing on it. Different muscle. Harder.

At this scale, good architecture is table stakes. You also need to influence without authority, build relationships across organisations and functions, and sell your ideas before you can build them. None of that comes from being the smartest person in a small room. It comes from operating in a room where you're not.

I haven't written a line of production code at Monday.com yet. I've studied the systems, I've talked to the teams, I have a thesis about where I can add leverage – but a thesis is not proof. I'll know more in six months than I do now.

## What I'm doing

I'm choosing an environment where the feedback loops are faster because the system is already live. The work I shipped at Honeycomb Software stands on its own. The simpler truth might be that I outgrew the room – and that's a harder thing to admit than a clean narrative about career strategy.

## What I think about career moves

Most career advice measures moves on one axis: up or lateral. The axis that matters is whether each role sharpens the lens for the next one.

This move is also an honest shift from a managerial position to an IC role at bigger scale. There's an industry narrative that careers should be a straight line upward – manager to director to VP, or senior to staff to principal. I don't buy it. The best careers aren't ladders. They're switchbacks – and the altitude you gain comes from the turns, not the straight lines. A system's real bottleneck is almost never technical – it's the decision that nobody wants to make, and architecture that ignores that will rot from the org chart inward. Leading teams taught me that. Going back to deeper technical work will make me a better leader the next time around. It's not a step back. It's a different angle on the same problem.

## What stays the same

I still care about systems that survive production and teams that multiply talent. MLOps should follow delivery reality, not tool fashion. The best engineering leaders are more technical, not less.

Agentic AI isn't replacing engineers – it's adding another floor to a building most people already can't see the basement of. We moved from assembler to C to Python. Now we're adding prompts, agent teams, scaffolding. Each layer means the lower ones get touched less. They don't stop mattering. When the agent fails at 3 AM, someone needs to understand every floor down to the foundation. That person only gets rarer and more expensive. I might be wrong – this might not be evolution but revolution, and the human finally leaves the loop. Not because AI got smarter, but because we decided we could afford to stop understanding what we built. I doubt we can.

The stage changes. The doctrine doesn't.

## What's next

I'll be writing about what I find. Presumably, some of it will be wrong. That's usually the interesting part.
